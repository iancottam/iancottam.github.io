Top tips: on coping with IT infrastructure outages

Ian Cottam (IT Relationship Manager), for e.g. Research IT Newsletter,
September issue 2015

 

No researcher expects many IT infrastructure outages these days: networks and email systems (etc.) are extremely reliable. However, events over the summer have caused me to think again about this topic.  Amongst other things, the following have been going on: all servers and systems in the Kilburn Data Centre have been moved out (mainly to modern, off-site facilities); network switches in several campus locations have been upgraded (so that, for example, data systems needing 1Gbps connectivity can be supported); and staff and postgraduate researchers from the School of Materials (MatSci Building) have been moved to various locations on North Campus (so their old building can be demolished ASAP as part of the 5+ years work to deliver the new Manchester Engineering Campus Development). All of the above have meant some unavoidable downtime of various IT systems.

 

Let’s consider a few scenarios, starting with a big one.

 

Scenario 1: Your building suffers loss of mains power

How would you cope with this, given that clearly you can’t even turn on your desktop? Well, all options require you having a laptop – some people might be able to cope with a tablet, but I find they are no real substitute for laptop computers yet. Laptops have battery power (obviously!). Reminder: keep your laptop topped up whilst working at your desktop, ditto your smartphone.

 

With your laptop you could wander over to a campus building that still has mains power – hence wifi network access – and use your laptop there. Starbucks, on North Campus, also has a strong eduroam wifi signal too. (Other expensive coffee shops are available.)

 

What if you wanted to remain in your office? You could connect your smartphone to a 4G/3G data network and then use it as a wifi personal hotspot to connect your laptop. (I use Apple devices and all of this is seamless once set up – you can even leave your phone in your pocket or bag and the connection can be easily, and transparently, made. I expect Android is similar, but have no direct experience to comment.) If your office starts to get dark – no mains lights remember – it is handy to have a laptop with a backlit keyboard. Apple MacBooks have these, and I expect some other manufacturers too.

 

Note that some Manchester systems and services will require you to run the Virtual Private Network (VPN) Client to access them wirelessly. For this reason, and others, eduroam is better than “UoM WiFi”, but in some cases VPN will still be needed.

 

Some researchers use a laptop all the time and, ideally, connect it to external screen, keyboard and mouse when working for long periods at the desk. If like me you use both a desktop and a laptop, you need to keep your user files in sync, otherwise when you switch back and forth you would be working with, at best, out of date files. I use cloud sync/storage for this: Dropbox+ncryptedCloud and Apple’s iCloud, but you could try relying on locally mounted network storage, such as Manchester’s P: drive system.

 

Hopefully, after just a few hours, power is restored, and you can revert to your normal configuration and network connection.

 

Scenario 2: The wired network stops working for you

This one has come up numerous times in the recent School of Materials move to North Campus. Clearly, researchers did not relocate before their new location had a working network. However, our wired network has specific Internet Protocol (IP) ranges per building. (It’s a little more complicated than that, but assume per building.) It takes time for IT staff to look up old IP addresses and substitute a new one that works in the destination location. Some people, this summer, were even further delayed because the movement of the equipment hosting the database that supports this function developed a temporary fault. Hence, to all intents and purposes, to staff and postgraduates the situation looked like a wired network infrastructure outage.

 

Some researchers coped better than others. There were two ‘lucky’ groups: those that use a laptop all the time and those that have Apple Mac desktops. What do they have in common? They could both use the near University-wide wifi system, until the wired network became available. (IPs are allocated dynamically on demand on the wifi network, compared with statically on wired.) I’m not sure if Apple Mac desktops are unique in coming with wifi cards as standard, but if I were a Windows PC desktop (only) user I would consider having a cheap plug-in wifi adapter for contingency.

 

A final “gotcha”, watch out, when moving buildings, for plugging your PC into a designated telephone socket – they look exactly like network sockets. Try another one in the same room to make sure, or plug in a telephone handset into the suspect one.

 

Scenario 3: some servers, printers and PCs fail to connect when the building’s network switches are upgraded

IT have seen this one several times this summer too; but why? The University has long had a regulation that users must not extend the network infrastructure themselves – for security reasons. However, older switches did not have the facility to detect and prevent such, whereas the new ones do and it is switched on by default. Hence, several users, as well as some IT Services support staff, were surprised when systems linked via various network extension technologies failed to connect. (Some Apple users may not even be aware they were/are doing this as several Apple devices – e.g. Time Capsules and Thunderbolt displays –support, in effect, daisy chaining of network ports.)

 

Clearly, the answer here is for all parties to identify such systems and discuss with IT’s Network Team how best to move forward. There may well be different solutions for the various cases.

 

Scenario 4: Network printer outage

Such outages could be caused by reasons explained above, or otherwise. It is always a good idea to have more than one printer set up in your list of available printers. If you only have access to one, come to a reciprocal arrangement with a research group close by.

 

I understand that the University is changing to an all “pull printing” approach, as the undergraduates already use, although they have to pay per page printed. Pull printing is a system whereby you print to a central server and then go to your local smart printer and enter your username and select that you want the job printed now and on this particular printer.

 

Scenario 5: University email service outage

We have not had a lengthy email service outage for quite a few years. Yet those who were here when we last did will recall what havoc it caused to the workings of the entire University. Even if you were not here then, it is easy to imagine.

 

At the very least, I would recommend having a personal email account, such as available from Google and other suppliers. If the University staff email system fails, there will likely be enough mail threads cached on your computer for you to switch them to your private email supplier. Be careful, though, not to put sensitive / data protection type data out on to the public Internet via such (likely USA based) email suppliers.
